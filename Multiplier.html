Iterative Multiplier

Multiplication is a fundamental operation in many programming algorithms, and it is particularly important in digital signal processing algorithms that spend most of their time multiplying values. With the increasing demand for processing signals in our media-heavy, highly connected Internet of Things (IoT) world, the need for efficient multiplication algorithms is becoming more critical. In this project, I present my work on synthesizing an iterative multiplier that supports the mul instruction defined in the PARCv1 Instruction Set Architecture (ISA). The goal is to develop a fully synthesizable multicore processor that includes this multiplier module. Specifically, I focus on two designs of an iterative multiplier: a fixed latency, 34 cycle iterative multiplier, which we call the "baseline design," and a variable latency iterative multiplier with bounds of 3 to 34 cycles, which I call the "alternative design." Finally, I evaluate the performance of both designs using a series of unit tests.

My baseline design follows the traditional multiplication algorithm taught in school. I shift the first operand left by one bit and the second operand right logically by one bit for each iteration, adding to an accumulator if the least significant bit (lsb) of the second operand is 1. This implementation closely follows this pseudocode, using three registers for the operands and the result, two shifters (logical left and logical right), and a multiplexor to decide whether to add to the accumulator. We also use a control-datapath split to implement the control logic and data flow.

To evaluate the performance of my baseline design, I run a series of unit tests. The tests show that the baseline design takes 34 clock cycles to complete a multiplication, which is relatively slow. Moreover, the hardware checks every bit of the second operand even if it is zero, which can be improved to reduce the execution time.

To address this issue, I propose an alternative design that exploits the structure of the operands to increase performance. The alternative design rolls over consecutive zeros by shifting more than one bit, if possible, and reduces the granularity of the number of zeros we shift over to powers of two. This approach reduces hardware costs while still increasing performance.

I present my implementation of the alternative design and evaluate its performance using the same series of unit tests. The results show that the alternative design consumes far fewer clock cycles than the baseline design, taking only 8.88 cycles per multiplication. However, it should be noted that the alternative design requires more area and energy than the baseline design. I also scale down the hardware use in the alternative design and find that it still performs significantly better than the baseline design, taking only 9.1 cycles per multiplication.

Furthermore, I discuss the modularity of both designs and how I could have used hierarchy to improve the implementation. I also discuss the advantages of encapsulation, which I used in my implementation by way of the val/rdy microprotocol.

In conclusion, my iterative multiplier designs can contribute to developing more efficient multiplication algorithms for digital signal processing. The alternative design significantly improves the performance of the traditional multiplication algorithm while reducing the execution time. However, it requires more area and energy than the baseline design. Future research could explore ways to optimize the hardware cost of the alternative design while maintaining its performance benefits.
